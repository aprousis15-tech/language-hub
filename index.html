📐 Greek Articles — The "the" and "a" Paradigm

Articles change with gender (masc/fem/neut), number (sing/plural), AND case (nominative/accusative/genitive). Memorise this grid — it unlocks everything.

Definite ("the")

MascFemNeut
Sing Nomοητο
Sing Accτοντηντο
Sing Genτουτηςτου
Plur Nomοιοιτα
Plur Accτουςτιςτα
Plur Genτωντωντων

Indefinite ("a / an")

MascFemNeut
Nomέναςμίαένα
Accένανμίαένα
Genενόςμιαςενός

No plural — Greek uses bare nouns or μερικά (some) instead.

Survival rules: (1) Match the article's gender to the noun. (2) Use Nominative for the subject — "ο άντρας έρχεται" (the man comes). (3) Use Accusative for the direct object — "βλέπω τον άντρα" (I see the man). (4) Use Genitive for "of / from / 's" — "το σπίτι του άντρα" (the man's house). (5) When in doubt, say it anyway — locals hear it, infer it, and reply.

How to tell if a noun is masculine, feminine, or neuter

Greek noun endings predict gender most of the time. Learn these three patterns and you'll pick the right article without thinking.

Masculine — ο
Most -ος, -ας, -ης endings
ο άντρας · ο φίλος · ο κόσμος · ο δρόμος · ο μήνας · ο μπαμπάς
Feminine — η
Most -η, -α endings
η μαμά · η αδερφή · η μπύρα · η οικογένεια · η ψυχή · η μέρα
Neuter — το
Most -ο, -ι, -μα endings
το νερό · το κρασί · το παιδί · το πρόβλημα · το σύστημα · το σπίτι

Agreement: article + adjective + noun all change together

  • All three must match in gender, number, and case. Masc: ο μεγάλος άντρας. Fem: η μεγάλη γυναίκα. Neut: το μεγάλο σπίτι.
  • When the noun's case changes, article and adjective change with it. Subject: ο μεγάλος άντρας έρχεται (the big man comes). Object: βλέπω τον μεγάλο άντρα (I see the big man). Possessor: το σπίτι του μεγάλου άντρα (the big man's house).
  • Greek uses articles where English doesn't: with country names (η Ελλάδα είναι όμορφη — Greece is beautiful), with personal names (ο Γιάννης ξέρει — Yannis knows), and with abstract / general nouns (μου αρέσει το παγωτό — I like ice cream).
  • Greek drops indefinite articles where English uses them: είμαι Αμερικανός = "I am American" (no "an"). είναι γιατρός = "she is a doctor".
  • Common gotchas: a few -ι words are NOT neuter (rare), and a few -ος words are feminine (η οδός = the street, η νήσος = the island). When you meet a noun, learn it WITH its article — "ο φίλος", not just "φίλος".

📚 Greek Rules & Endings

Your running cheat sheet — the rules and endings worth memorizing. Glance here before any speaking practice.

🔑 Key Rules to Remember
να separates verbs
When you have two verbs back-to-back, να links them — and the second verb takes its closed form.
Θέλω να δω φωτογραφία — I want to see a photo (not βλέπω → δω)
Θέλω να ξυπνήσω στις 8 — I want to wake up at 8 (not ξυπνάω → ξυπνήσω)
δεν vs. όχι — two different "no"s
δεν only goes with verbs. όχι goes with everything else (nouns, adverbs, adjectives).
Δεν είναι εδώ — is not here (verb → δεν)
Όχι σήμερα — not today (adverb → όχι)
Double negative — always required
Greek does not drop the δεν when you also have ποτέ / κανένας / τίποτα. Keep both.
Ποτέ δεν πίνω καφέ — I never drink coffee
Κανένας δεν είναι εδώ — No one is here
Δεν θέλω τίποτα — I don't want anything
Time expressions: τον/την/το vs. στις
Time expressions take the article (τον/την/το) — except clock times and dates, which take στις.
EnglishGreekRule
on Mondayτην Δευτέραarticle
in Julyτον Ιούλιοarticle
in the afternoonτο απόγευμαarticle
in 2026το 2026article
at 9 o'clockστις εννέα η ώραclock → στις

Adjectives match the noun's gender
The adjective ending changes to match the gender (and number) of the noun it describes.
Η Ναταλία είναι κουρασμένη — fem. noun → fem. adjective ending -η
Ο Νίκος είναι κουρασμένος — masc. noun → masc. ending -ος
Open vs. closed verb forms
Most verbs have an open form (the dictionary form, used in habitual present) and a closed form (used after να, θα, αν).
Ξυπνάω κάθε μέρα — open, habitual
Θέλω να ξυπνήσω — closed after να
Θα σου δείξω — closed after θα (from δείχνω)
πότε vs. ποτέ — accent changes the meaning
πότε (accent on the first syllable) = when
ποτέ (accent on the last syllable) = never
Demonstratives need the article in between
In Greek, "this/that + noun" is this + the + noun. The article sits between the demonstrative and the noun, and both agree with the noun's gender, number, and case.
✅ αυτό το βιβλίο — this book  ·  ❌ αυτό βιβλίο
✅ αυτή την εβδομάδα — this week  ·  ❌ αυτή εβδομάδα
✅ αυτός ο άντρας — this man  ·  ❌ αυτός άντρας
πολύ vs. πολλή/πολύς — adverb or adjective?
πολύ (invariable adverb) modifies verbs and adjectives — it never changes form.
πολύς / πολλή / πολύ / πολλοί / πολλές / πολλά (adjective) modifies nouns — agrees with gender and number.
Είμαι πολύ κουρασμένος — adverb modifying adjective
Δουλεύω πολύ — adverb modifying verb
Πολλή δουλειά — adjective modifying fem. noun
Πολλά παιδιά — adjective modifying neut. plural noun
Test: what does it modify? Verb or adjective → πολύ. Noun → adjective form that agrees.
αυτός vs. εκείνος — near vs. far
αυτός / αυτή / αυτό = this (near, or default when distance doesn't matter).
εκείνος / εκείνη / εκείνο = that (far, or contrasting "that one over there, not this one").
Αυτό το βιβλίο είναι καλό — This book is good.
Εκείνο το βιβλίο, όχι αυτό — That book, not this one.
In casual spoken Greek, αυτός covers both. Reach for εκείνος only when you're explicitly contrasting near vs. far.
🧩 Cases — the article changes by role
The article shifts depending on the noun's role in the sentence.
CaseMasc.Fem.Neut.Used when…
Nominativeοητοdescribing the subject
Genitiveτουτηςτουpossession ("of the…")
Accusativeτοντηντοdirect object, after prepositions
Masculine drops the -ς in accusative: ο πατέρας (nom.) → τον πατέρα (acc.). Same for ο φίλος → τον φίλο.
Prepositions like σε contract with the article: σε + τον = στον, σε + την = στην, σε + το = στο.
🔤 Gender Endings — spot it from the ending
Masculine: -ος, -ας, -ης
ο άνθρωπος (o ánthropos) — the person
ο πατέρας (o patéras) — the father
ο μαθητής (o mathitís) — the student
Feminine: -α, -η (and rarely -ος)
η μητέρα (i mitéra) — the mother
η αδελφή (i adelfí) — the sister
η οδός (i odós) — the street — exception, looks masculine but is feminine
Neuter: -ο, -ι, -μα
το βιβλίο (to vivlío) — the book
το παιδί (to pedí) — the child
το πρόβλημα (to próvlima) — the problem
-μα words are always neuter: πράγμα (thing), πρόβλημα (problem), θέμα (topic), μάθημα (lesson)…
⚡ Quick rule of thumb
Ends in -ς → probably masculine
Ends in -α or -η → probably feminine
Ends in -ο, -ι, or -μα → probably neuter
All feminine words end in η as their article, even when the noun itself ends in -α (like η μητέρα) or -ος (like η οδός).

🎯 Macro Patterns — Fix 80% of Errors

From the 11/05 writing-practice feedback. Internalize these seven and dozens of future mistakes vanish.

1. Every "ee" sound is a spelling decision
Greek has 5+ ways to write "ee": η, ι, υ, ει, οι. Each word has a fixed spelling — there's no rule, just memory. Pause every time you write an "ee" sound; don't auto-default to ι.
είναι (ει) · σήμερα (η) · δύσκολη (υ) · εκεί (ει) · νωρίς (ι)
2. Verb endings are non-negotiable
Present tense:
1st person = -ω (Έχω, not Έχο)
2nd person = -εις (έχεις)
3rd person = -ει (έχει)
Mixing 2nd and 3rd person changes WHO is doing the action. κάνεις = "you do." κάνει = "he/she does." Same root, different subject.
3. Gender agreement is a chain
Article + adjective + noun must all match in gender and number. If you know one piece, the others follow.
GenderArticleAdjectiveNoun
Feminineη / μίανέαφίλη
Masculineο / έναςνέοςφίλος
Neuterτο / ένανέοπαιδί
Gender carries across sentences too. If you said ο καιρός last sentence, "it's bad" continues with masc.: Είναι κακός — not κακό.
4. δ and θ are different sounds
δ = soft "th" as in this.
θ = hard "th" as in thin.
"Don't / not" is δεν — soft th. Writing θεν says a nonsense word. Same trap: δουλειά (work), not θουλειά.
5. σ becomes ς at the end of every word
Middle of a word: σ.
End of a word: ς.
No exceptions. καιρός, φίλος, μαθητής, νωρίς.
6. Greek has no present continuous
English "is + verb-ing" → Greek simple present. There is no είναι + verb construction.
"She is traveling" → ταξιδεύει (not είναι ταξιδεύει).
"I am eating" → τρώω. "He is sleeping" → κοιμάται.
This is the single most common English-thinking error. Drop "is" whenever you're tempted to add it before a verb.

📖 Foundations of Greek Grammar

Beginner-level explainers. Start here if you've never built a Greek sentence from first principles — these walk through the WHY behind everything you've been drilling.

Verbs carry more information inside the word in Greek than in English. The ending of the verb tells you WHO is doing the action — so the pronoun is optional. Learn one verb's six person-endings and you've cracked the system. Click any part below to expand.

A verb is a word that names an action or a state of being. In English: I walk, you eat, she is, they sleep. Greek works the same way — every sentence has a verb at its core.

But Greek verbs carry far more information inside the word itself than English verbs do. This is the single most important thing to internalize.

In English, the verb "walk" tells you almost nothing on its own. You need a separate pronoun:

I walk, you walk, we walk, they walk — same word "walk," four different meanings.

In Greek, the verb itself tells you who's doing it. The ending changes:

GreekEnglishWhat the ending means
περπατάωI walk-ω = "I"
περπατάςyou walk-ας = "you"
περπατάειhe/she/it walks-αει = "he/she/it"
περπατάμεwe walk-αμε = "we"
περπατάτεyou all walk-ατε = "you all"
περπατάνεthey walk-ανε = "they"

You don't say εγώ περπατάω the way you say "I walk" in English. The εγώ is optional and usually dropped, because the -ω ending already tells you "I."

The first mental shift: in Greek, the ending of the verb IS the subject pronoun. You're not learning a verb — you're learning six forms of every verb.

Every verb in every language can be conjugated for six grammatical "persons." Memorize these labels — every Greek grammar book uses them.

When you look up a verb in a Greek dictionary, you find the 1st person singular present form. So the dictionary entry is "I ___," not "to ___."

"To walk" in a Greek dictionary appears as περπατάω, literally "I walk."

Greek has no infinitive ("to walk") at all in modern usage — it was lost over the centuries. We'll see what replaced it in Part 8.

This is the single most important new concept. Aspect = the difference between ongoing/habitual action and a single completed event.

English has hints. Compare: "I was reading when she called" (ongoing) vs. "I read the book yesterday" (completed). "I eat fish" (habitual) vs. "I ate the fish" (one event).

English makes these distinctions partially. Greek bakes the distinction into the verb stem itself. Every action verb has TWO completely separate stems:

Imperfective form — for ongoing, habitual, repeated, or in-progress action. Use when: action is happening now, happens regularly, or has no defined endpoint. This is the form you've been learning: γράφω.

Perfective form (aorist) — for a single completed event, viewed as a whole. Use when: one bounded action, future actions that happen once (with θα), past actions completed, after να. Different stem of the same verb: γράψω.

Critical: in isolation, γράψω doesn't mean anything specific — it's a stem waiting for context. You almost never use a perfective form alone. You use it after θα (will), να (to/that), or with the past prefix ε-.

Tense = when the action happened. Six tenses in Greek; here are the four essentials.

Present tense — imperfective stem + basic endings.

  • γράφω — I write / I'm writing
  • τρώω — I eat / I'm eating
  • περπατάω — I walk / I'm walking

Future continuous — θα + imperfective stem. "I'll be doing it / I'll do it habitually."

  • θα γράφω — I'll be writing (ongoing)
  • θα τρώω — I'll be eating (regularly)

Future simple — θα + perfective (aorist) stem. "I'll do it once." This is the everyday future.

  • θα γράψω — I'll write (one time)
  • θα φάω — I'll eat (one specific meal)
  • θα πάω — I'll go (one trip)

Aorist past — ε- prefix + aorist stem + past endings (-α, -ες, -ε, -αμε, -ατε, -αν).

Example with γράφω → aorist stem γράψ-:

  • έγραψα (I wrote)
  • έγραψες (you wrote)
  • έγραψε (he/she wrote)
  • γράψαμε (we wrote) — ε- disappears, stress moves
  • γράψατε (you all wrote)
  • έγραψαν (they wrote)

Critical irregular aorists — drill these as fixed shapes:

  • πήγα — I went
  • είδα — I saw
  • έφαγα — I ate
  • ήπια — I drank
  • είπα — I said
  • πήρα — I took
  • έκανα — I did/made
  • ήμουν — I was
  • ήθελα — I wanted
  • βρήκα — I found

Voice = who is doing what to whom.

Active voice — subject does the action to something else.
γράφω ένα γράμμα — I write a letter.
95% of your verbs are active.

Passive voice — subject receives the action.
το γράμμα γράφεται — the letter is being written.
English uses "is being ___ed." Greek uses different endings (the -μαι family).

Middle voice — the action happens to/for the subject themselves. This doesn't exist in English.
κοιμάμαι (I sleep) — sleeping happens to/within me.
ντύνομαι (I get dressed) — I dress myself.
σκέφτομαι (I think) — thinking happens inside me.

In Greek, the same -μαι endings are used for both middle and passive. Context disambiguates.

Deponent verbs — look middle, mean active: εύχομαι (I wish), έρχομαι (I come), ασχολούμαι (I'm occupied with). Memorize them with their -μαι ending — don't try to derive them.

Mood = the speaker's stance: stating a fact? Wishing? Commanding?

Indicative mood — plain statements of fact. What you've been using. γράφω (I write), πήγαμε (we went), θα φάω (I'll eat).

Subjunctive mood — possibility, wish, purpose, "to ___." The big new one.
Greek lost its infinitive. The subjunctive replaces it. When English uses "to ___" or "that ___" or "should ___," Greek uses να + a verb form.

  • θέλω να φάω — I want to eat
  • πρέπει να πάμε — we must go
  • μπορώ να βοηθήσω — I can help
  • θέλω να μάθω ελληνικά — I want to learn Greek
  • για να καταλάβω — so that I understand
You'll use this constantly. Every "I want to / need to / can / try to" in English maps to θέλω/πρέπει/μπορώ/προσπαθώ + να + verb.

After να, you use either imperfective or perfective depending on aspect:

  • θέλω να γράφω (I want to be writing / write habitually) — imperfective
  • θέλω να γράψω (I want to write once, complete it) — perfective

Imperative mood — commands. See the dedicated ❗ Imperatives section.

Every Greek verb carries answers to five questions simultaneously:

  1. Who? (person/number)
  2. When? (tense — present? future? past?)
  3. Process or event? (aspect — imperfective or perfective stem?)
  4. Who's acting on whom? (voice — active, middle, passive)
  5. Speaker's attitude? (mood — stating, wishing, commanding)

Example: Θέλω να φάμε μαζί αύριο. (I want us to eat together tomorrow.)

  • θέλω — 1st sing present indicative active of θέλω
  • να — subjunctive marker
  • φάμε — 1st plural aorist subjunctive of τρώω
  • μαζί — together
  • αύριο — tomorrow

Two verbs, both fully decoded once you know the system.

Absolute minimum to drill before adding anything else:

  1. The six person endings of Family A (-ω, -εις, -ει, -ουμε, -ετε, -ουν). Drill on five verbs until automatic.
  2. The full conjugation of είμαι present and past. Irregular and unavoidable.
  3. The aorist pairs. γράφω ↔ γράψω, τρώω ↔ φάω, πίνω ↔ πιω. Drill in both directions.
  4. The θα + aorist = future pattern. θα γράψω, θα φάω, θα πάω.
  5. The θέλω/μπορώ/πρέπει + να + aorist pattern. Unlocks every "I want to ___" sentence.

Once these are automatic: add past tense (ε- prefix), imperatives, and the full middle voice. 4-6 week roadmap at 20 minutes/day.

Nouns are the people, places, things, and ideas in a sentence. Every Greek noun has a gender (masc / fem / neut) — and the gender controls everything: which article it takes, what adjective form goes with it, how it changes shape across cases. Once you know the gender of a noun, the rest follows mechanically.

A noun is a word for a person, place, thing, or idea. House, woman, idea, Athens.

In English, nouns are simple. They change for plural (one cat → two cats) and that's it. Greek nouns carry FOUR pieces of information inside the word:

  1. Gender — masculine, feminine, or neuter (assigned, not biological)
  2. Number — singular or plural
  3. Case — nominative, accusative, genitive, vocative (the noun's role in the sentence)
  4. Article — almost every noun comes with one (the / a)

So a single Greek noun like τον φίλο tells you: it's masculine, singular, accusative, and definite. The English equivalent "the friend" only tells you "definite."

Key habit: when you learn a new Greek noun, always memorize it WITH its article. Not σπίτι — learn το σπίτι. The article tells you the gender, and the gender tells you everything else.

Every Greek noun is masculine, feminine, or neuter. Gender is grammatical, not biological — το κορίτσι (the girl) is neuter, η οδός (the street) is feminine.

Adjectives describe nouns (big, red, tired, beautiful). In Greek, adjectives change form to match the noun in gender, number, and case. Once you can identify a noun's gender, the adjective ending follows mechanically. The adjective is the GLUE that binds the article and noun together — get them to agree and your Greek immediately stops sounding broken.

In English, "big" is "big" no matter what noun follows: a big house, a big car, a big dog, big houses. The adjective never changes.

In Greek, every adjective has at least THREE forms — one for each gender — and changes for plural and case too:

NounGreekAdj. ending
A big friend (m)ένας μεγάλος φίλος-ος (masc)

A big problem (n)ένα μεγάλο πρόβλημα-ο (neut)

Same word, three endings. The chain is article + adjective + noun, and all three must agree.

The rule of three: if you know the noun's gender, the adjective ending follows automatically. Mismatch and your speech reads as broken Greek even if everything else is right.

  • νέος / νέα / νέο — new (irregular: fem ends in -α)
  • κουρασμένος / κουρασμένη / κουρασμένο — tired
  • ωραίος / ωραία / ωραίο — nice / beautiful (fem in -α)
  • δύσκολος / δύσκολη / δύσκολο — difficult

Pattern variant: some adjectives use feminine -α instead of -η (νέα, ωραία, πλούσια). Just memorize which is which when you learn the adjective.

When you say "X is Y" — the adjective still agrees with X, even with the verb είναι in between. είναι is gender-invisible; the adjective looks back at the subject.

  • Ο καιρός είναι κακός. — The weather is bad. (καιρός is masc → κακός)
  • Η εργασία είναι δύσκολη. — The homework is difficult. (εργασία is fem → δύσκολη)
  • Το μάθημα είναι δύσκολο. — The lesson is difficult. (μάθημα is neut → δύσκολο)
Gender carries across sentences too. If the conversation is about ο καιρός, and you say "It's bad," that "bad" must be masculine: Είναι κακός. NOT κακό (neuter default), even though English "it" is neuter.

Default order: article + adjective + noun.

  • η ωραία μέρα — the beautiful day
  • ένα μικρό σπίτι — a small house
  • οι κουρασμένοι φίλοι — the tired friends

Adjective AFTER the noun happens in two cases:

  1. Predicate position (with είναι): Η μέρα είναι ωραία.
  2. Emphasis or definite article repetition: η μέρα η ωραία = "the day — the beautiful one." Rare in normal speech.

  2. Adjective ending matches the noun. Article + adjective + noun all in the same gender. Mismatch = broken Greek.

Adverbs are the easiest part of Greek for English speakers. They don't change form — no gender, no number, no case. Memorize the word once and you're done. They modify verbs, adjectives, or whole sentences.

An adverb modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. In English: slowly, often, very, today, here.

Adverbs answer questions like:

  • How? — καλά (well), γρήγορα (quickly), σιγά (slowly)
  • When? — σήμερα (today), αύριο (tomorrow), τώρα (now)
  • Where? — εδώ (here), εκεί (there)
  • How often? — πάντα (always), συχνά (often), ποτέ (never)
  • How much? — πολύ (very), λίγο (a little)
The good news: Greek adverbs are INVARIABLE. They never agree with anything. Once you know γρήγορα, that's it — no plural form, no feminine form, no case changes.

Greek word order is more flexible than English. Adverbs can usually go in several positions:

  • Σήμερα δουλεύω. (At-the-start emphasis)
  • Δουλεύω σήμερα. (At-the-end, standard)
  • Δουλεύω πολύ σήμερα. (Mid-sentence, between verb and another modifier)

  • Frequency adverbs typically come right before the verb: Πάντα ξυπνάω νωρίς.

  5. Negation pairs. ποτέ / κανείς / τίποτα / πουθενά ALWAYS take δεν before the verb.
  6. Build a daily set: the 30-or-so adverbs in Part 3 cover ~90% of everyday conversation.

Imperatives are the form you use to give commands or instructions ("Come!", "Wait!", "Tell me!"). In Greek they're a special mood of the verb with their own endings. Comprehension matters more than production at first — you'll hear imperatives constantly even if you rarely produce them.

An imperative is a command, request, instruction, or invitation directed at someone. In English: Come! Wait. Sit down. Please pass the salt.

Greek imperatives exist in only TWO forms — 2nd singular and 2nd plural. You can only command someone you're addressing directly; you can't issue an imperative to yourself or a third person (for that, Greek uses subjunctive with να).

Why μη and not δεν? δεν is for indicative statements ("I don't go"). μη(ν) is for the subjunctive / non-real mood. Imperatives, prohibitions, hypotheticals — all use μη(ν).

μη vs μην: the ν is dropped before consonants in modern Greek, kept before vowels. Μη μιλάς (consonant after) vs Μην ανησυχείς (vowel after).

Direct imperatives can sound blunt. To soften:

(1) Use the plural/formal form: Δώστε μου (give me, formal) sounds politer than Δώσε μου.

(2) Use a question with μπορείς να / μπορείτε να:

  • Μπορείτε να μου δώσετε τον λογαριασμό; — Can you give me the bill?
  • Μπορείς να με βοηθήσεις; — Can you help me?

(3) Use θα ήθελα να ("I would like to"):

  • Θα ήθελα να δω το μενού. — I'd like to see the menu.
  • Θα ήθελα έναν καφέ, παρακαλώ. — I'd like a coffee, please.

(4) Add παρακαλώ:

  • Πες μου, παρακαλώ. — Tell me, please.
  • Περίμενε λίγο, παρακαλώ. — Wait a moment, please.
In professional and service contexts, default to μπορείτε να + verb. It's never wrong and always polite.
  1. Comprehension first, production second. Recognize the 15 daily imperatives in Part 3 — don't worry about producing them.
  2. Singular vs plural. Drill the pair: Έλα! / Ελάτε! — one informal, one formal.
  3. Negative = μη(ν) + subjunctive. NOT δεν. Drill Μη μιλάς / Μην ανησυχείς as fixed shapes.
  4. Polite version: μπορείς/μπορείτε να + verb. Universal request softener. Use it freely.
  5. παρακαλώ added to ANY command. Even a curt Πες μου becomes polite with , παρακαλώ tagged on.
